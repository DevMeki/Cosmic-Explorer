02. The Morning Star

Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun. It is named after the Roman goddess of love and beauty. As the brightest natural object in Earth's night sky after the Moon.

The Hottest Planet

Venus is often called Earth's "sister planet" because of their similar size, mass, density, and bulk composition. However, it is radically different from Earth in other respects. It has the densest atmosphere of the four terrestrial planets, consisting of more than 96% carbon dioxide. The atmospheric pressure at the planet's surface is 92 times that of Earth, or roughly the pressure found 3,000 ft (900 m) underwater on Earth. This atmosphere trap's heat in a runaway greenhouse effect, making Venus the hottest planet in the Solar System, with surface temperatures of at least 864 degrees Fahrenheit (462 degrees Celsius).

Venus is shrouded by an opaque layer of highly reflective clouds of sulfuric acid, preventing its surface from being seen from space in visible light. It may have had water oceans in the past, but these would have vaporized as the temperature rose due to a runaway greenhouse effect. The hydrogen probably escaped into heavy amounts of solar wind, with little oxygen remained. The surface of Venus is a dry desertscape interspersed with slab-like rocks and is periodically resurfaced by volcanism. The number of volcanoes on Venus is estimated to be over 1,600, although most are likely extinct.

One of the most peculiar aspects of Venus is its rotation. It rotates on its axis in the opposite direction to most other planets (retrograde rotation). This means the Sun rises in the west and sets in the east. Furthermore, its rotation is incredibly slow; a single day on Venus (one rotation on its axis) lasts 243 Earth days, which is actually longer than its year (it takes 225 Earth days to orbit the Sun). This makes it the only planet where a day is longer than a year.

Exploration of Venus has been intense but often short-lived due to the crushing pressure and searing heat. The Soviet Union's Venera program achieved the only soft landings on the surface, with probes surviving only for an hour or two before succumbing to the harsh conditions. These missions returned the only images we have from the surface of Venus, showing a yellow, rocky, desolate world. NASA's Magellan mission orbited Venus in the early 1990s, using radar to map 98% of the surface through the thick clouds, revealing vast volcanic plains and highland regions.

(Content Extension...) The atmosphere of Venus offers a cautionary tale for Earth. It demonstrates the catastrophic potential of the greenhouse effect. Scientists study Venus to understand how a planet that started so similar to Earth could end up so different. This comparative planetology is vital for modeling climate change on our own planet. Additionally, the upper atmosphere of Venus is actually surprisingly Earth-like in terms of temperature and pressure. Some scientists have even proposed that microbial life could exist in the clouds, floating high above the scorching surface.

In recent years, the discovery of phosphine gas in the cloud decks of Venus sparked renewed interest in the possibility of life. Phosphine is a biosignature on Earth, produced by anaerobic bacteria. While the detection is still debated, it has spurred plans for new missions to sample the atmosphere directly. NASA's DAVINCI+ and VERITAS missions, and ESA's EnVision, are set to launch in the coming decade to unravel the mysteries of our twin world.

Human colonization of Venus is a popular topic in science fiction but presents immense engineering challenges. However, "floating cities" in the upper atmosphere are a chemically and physically feasible concept, arguably more so than terraforming Mars. At 50 kilometers up, there is plenty of solar energy, the atmosphere provides radiation shielding, and the gravity is nearly identical to Earth's. The primary challenge would be the sulfuric acid in the clouds, requiring acid-resistant materials.

The universe is vast and expanding. Every direction we look, we see galaxies moving away from us. This cosmic expansion, driven by the mysterious dark energy, paints a picture of a universe that is both dynamic and ancient. We measure distances in light-years, the distance light travels in a year—about 5.88 trillion miles. The nearest star to our Sun, Proxima Centauri, is over 4 light-years away. To reach it with current technology would take tens of thousands of years. This scale is humbling. It reminds us of the fragility of our own pale blue dot, suspended in a sunbeam. It underscores the importance of cherishing and preserving our home planet, the only place we know of that can harbor life.

But we are not deterred. We develop new propulsion systems—ion drives, solar sails, potentially even nuclear thermal rockets—to speed our passage through the dark. We design self-sustaining habitats for long-duration spaceflight. We study the physiological effects of microgravity and radiation on the human body. We are preparing to become a multi-planetary species. This is not science fiction; it is the roadmap for the next century of human history.
