04. The Red Planet

Mars

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun and the second-smallest planet in the Solar System. It is often referred to as the "Red Planet".

The Next Frontier

Mars is a dusty, cold, desert world with a very thin atmosphere. There is no standing water on the surface today, but many science missions—orbiters, landers, and rovers—have shown that Mars was once a wet world with a thicker atmosphere, capable of supporting life. The surface of Mars is reminiscent of both the impact craters of the Moon and the volcanoes, valleys, deserts, and polar ice caps of Earth. The days and seasons are comparable to those of Earth, because the rotation period and tilt of the rotational axis relative to the ecliptic plane are similar.

Mars is the site of Olympus Mons, the largest volcano and highest known mountain on any planet in the Solar System, and of Valles Marineris, one of the largest canyons in the Solar System. The smooth Borealis basin in the northern hemisphere covers 40% of the planet and may be a giant impact feature. Mars has two moons, Phobos and Deimos, which are small and irregularly shaped. These may be captured asteroids, similar to 5261 Eureka, a Mars trojan.

We have sent more robots to Mars than any other continent outside of Earth. The Curiosity rover has been exploring crater Gale since 2012, analyzing rocks and climate. The Perseverance rover, which landed in 2021, is hunting for signs of ancient microbial life and collecting samples for future return to Earth. The Ingenuity helicopter, a technology demonstration, proved that powered flight is possible in the thin Martian atmosphere.

Terraforming Mars is a hypothetical process by which the Martian climate, surface, and known properties would be deliberately altered with the goal of making it habitable by human beings and other terrestrial life. The concept relies on the assumption that the environment of a planet can be altered through artificial means. Feasible methods usually involve "thickening" the atmosphere with greenhouse gases to warm it, which would also increase atmospheric pressure.

The search for life on Mars is the primary driver of current exploration. While the surface is harsh, the subsurface might still harbor liquid water aquifers where extremophiles could survive. The discovery of potential briny water seeps on steep slopes during the summer months (Recurring Slope Lineae) tantalized scientists, though the exact nature of these features is still debated. Methane plumes detected in the atmosphere also hint at active biological or geological processes, though the source remains elusive.
