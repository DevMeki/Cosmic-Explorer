06. The Ringed Jewel

Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second-largest in the Solar System, after Jupiter. It is a gas giant with an average radius of about nine and a half times that of Earth.

Jewel of the Solar System

Saturn is most famous for its ring system, which is composed of ice particles with a smaller amount of rocky debris and dust. The rings extend from 6,630 km to 120,700 km outward from Saturn's equator and average approximately 20 meters in thickness. There are two main theories regarding the origin of the rings. One theory is that they are remnants of a destroyed moon of Saturn. The second is that they are left over from the original nebular material from which Saturn formed.

Saturn's interior is probably similar to Jupiter's, with a small rocky core surrounded by a deep layer of metallic hydrogen, an intermediate layer of liquid hydrogen and liquid helium, and finally a gaseous outer layer. Saturn has a pale yellow hue due to ammonia crystals in its upper atmosphere. Wind speeds on Saturn can reach 1,800 km/h (1,100 mph), higher than on Jupiter, but not as high as those on Neptune.

Saturn has 146 known moons, the most of any planet. Titan, the largest, is the only moon in the Solar System to have a substantial atmosphere, and the only known body in space other than Earth where clear evidence of stable bodies of surface liquid has been found. These lakes are not water, however, but liquid methane and ethane. Enceladus, another moon, has geysers of water ice erupting from its south pole, hinting at a subsurface ocean that could potentially harbor life.

The Cassini-Huygens mission (2004-2017) transformed our understanding of Saturn. It orbited the planet for 13 years, sending back stunning images of the rings and moons. The Huygens probe successfully landed on Titan in 2005, revealing a world with river channels and lakes, eerily similar to Earth yet alien in its chemistry.

(Content Extension...) Saturn is lighter than water. If you had a bathtub big enough to hold it, Saturn would float. This is because it is made mostly of hydrogen and helium, the two lightest elements in the universe. Its low density and rapid rotation cause it to flatten at the poles and bulge at the equator more than any other planet.

The "Hexagon" is a persisting cloud pattern around the north pole of Saturn, located at about 78 degrees N. The sides of the hexagon are about 14,500 km (9,000 mi) long, which is more than the diameter of Earth. It rotates with a period of 10h 39m 24s, the same period as Saturn's radio emissions from its interior. The origin of the pattern is a matter of much speculation. Most regular polygons are unstable in fluid dynamics, so explaining how such a rigid shape can persist in a turbulent atmosphere is a major challenge for planetary scientists.

The rings of Saturn are not solid. They are made of billions of individual particles, ranging in size from specks of dust to mountains. They are held in place by the delicate balance of Saturn's gravity and their own orbital momentum, as well as the gravitational influence of "shepherd moons" that orbit within or near the rings, keeping the particles confined.
