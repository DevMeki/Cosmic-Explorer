03. The Blue Planet

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only astronomical object known to harbor life. It is the densest planet in the Solar System.

Our Cosmic Home

Earth is the largest and densest of the four inner, terrestrial planets. About 71% of Earth's surface is covered with water, mostly by its oceans. The remaining 29% is land consisting of continents and islands. Earth's surface helps to keep its atmosphere warm and is essential for life as we know it. The interior of Earth is active, with a solid iron inner core, a liquid outer core that generates Earth's magnetic field, and a convecting mantle that drives plate tectonics.

Earth formed over 4.5 billion years ago. Within the first billion years, life appeared in its oceans and began to affect Earth's atmosphere and surface, promoting the proliferation of aerobic as well as anaerobic organisms. Some geological evidence indicates that life may have arisen as much as 4.1 billion years ago. Since then, the combination of Earth's distance from the Sun, physical properties, and geological history have allowed life to evolve and thrive. Biodiversity has undergone periods of expansion, interrupted by mass extinction events. Over 99% of all species that ever lived on Earth are extinct, yet the planet is currently home to over 8 billion humans and millions of other species.

(Content Extension...) Observing Earth from space is known as the "Overview Effect," a cognitive shift reported by astronauts. Seeing the planet as a single, fragile sphere floating in the void creates a sense of unity and a desire to protect it. Satellites orbiting Earth provide crucial data on weather patterns, climate change, deforestation, and urban development. Remote sensing is one of our most powerful tools for understanding the complex interconnected systems that sustain our biosphere.

The Anthropocene is a proposed geological epoch dating from the commencement of significant human impact on Earth's geology and ecosystems. Humans have altered the planet's surface, atmosphere, and oceans to a degree that will be detectable in the geological record for millions of years. Climate change, driven by the burning of fossil fuels, poses the single greatest threat to our civilization and the natural world. Rising sea levels, extreme weather events, and ocean acidification are reshaping our environment.
