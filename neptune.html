08. The Windy Giant

Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and farthest-known Solar planet from the Sun. In the Solar System, it is the fourth-largest planet by diameter.

The Last Outpost

Neptune is dark, cold, and whipped by supersonic winds. It is the only planet in our solar system not visible to the naked eye and the first predicted by mathematics before its discovery. In 2011 Neptune completed its first 165-year orbit since its discovery in 1846. Neptune is an ice giant, similar to Uranus, but slightly smaller and more massive. Its atmosphere is made of hydrogen, helium, and methane. The methane gives Neptune the same blue color as Uranus, but Neptune is a much deeper, richer blue, suggesting there is an unknown component in the atmosphere that intensifies the color.

Neptune's winds are the strongest in the Solar System, reaching reach speeds of 1,200 miles per hour (2,000 kilometers per hour). These winds push the frozen clouds of natural gas around the planet at breakneck speeds. Voyager 2 spotted a Great Dark Spot in the southern hemisphere in 1989, a storm large enough to contain the entire Earth. When the Hubble Space Telescope looked for it five years later, it had vanished, only for a new dark spot to appear in the northern hemisphere.

Neptune has 16 known moons. Triton is the largest and arguably the most interesting. It orbits the planet in the opposite direction to the planet's rotation (retrograde orbit), suggesting it is a captured object from the Kuiper Belt. Triton is extremely cold, with temperatures dipping to -391 degrees Fahrenheit (-235 degrees Celsius). Despite the cold, Voyager 2 discovered geysers of liquid nitrogen spewing 5 miles into its thin atmosphere.

(Content Extension...) Neptune exerts a profound gravitational influence on the Kuiper Belt, a region of icy bodies beyond its orbit. Pluto, for example, is in a 2:3 orbital resonance with Neptune, meaning for every three orbits Neptune makes around the Sun, Pluto makes two. This resonance protects Pluto from colliding with the giant planet. Many other "Plutinos" share this resonant orbit.

Understanding the internal structure of Neptune is a challenge. It likely has a core of rock and ice, surrounded by a mantle of water, ammonia, and methane ices. Scientists call this "ice," but at the extreme temperatures and pressures inside Neptune, it acts more like a hot, dense fluid. Some models suggest diamonds could rain down through this mantle, accumulating around the core.

Since the Voyager 2 flyby in 1989, no spacecraft has visited Neptune. It remains one of the least explored targets in the solar system. Future concepts, such as a Neptune Orbiter or even a probe to dive into its atmosphere, are discussed by space agencies, but the long travel time (over a decade) and high cost make such missions difficult to greenlight. Until then, we must rely on telescopes like Hubble and Webb to monitor its weather and moons from afar.
