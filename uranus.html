07. The Sideways Planet

Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun. It has the third-largest planetary radius and fourth-largest planetary mass in the Solar System.

The Ice Giant

Uranus is an ice giant, composed mostly of fluids (water, methane, and ammonia) above a small rocky core. It gets its blue-green, or cyan, color from methane gas in the atmosphere, which absorbs red light and reflects blue-green light. It is arguably the weirdest planet in the Solar System. Its axis of rotation is tilted sideways, nearly into the plane of its solar orbit. Its north and south poles lie where most other planets have their equators. This unique tilt causes the most extreme seasons in the solar system. For nearly a quarter of each Uranian year, the sun shines directly over each pole, plunging the other half of the planet into a 21-year-long, dark winter.

The atmosphere of Uranus is the coldest in the Solar System, with a minimum temperature of 49 K (-224 degrees C; -371 degrees F). It has a complex, layered cloud structure, with water thought to make up the lowest clouds and methane the uppermost layer of clouds. The interior of Uranus is mainly composed of ices and rock.

Voyager 2 is the only spacecraft to have visited Uranus. It flew by in 1986, revealing a largely featureless, pale blue ball, unlike the stormy Jupiter and Saturn. However, more recent observations from the Hubble Space Telescope have shown that as Uranus approached its equinox in 2007, it developed bright storms and distinct cloud bands in its atmosphere.

(Content Extension...) Uranus has 27 known moons, named after characters from the works of William Shakespeare and Alexander Pope. This naming convention breaks from the tradition of using figures from Greek or Roman mythology. Titania and Oberon are the largest. Miranda, a smaller moon, has a bizarrely fractured surface with giant canyons and patchwork terrain, suggesting it was shattered by a massive impact and reassembled, or experienced intense tectonic activity.

The magnetic field of Uranus is also peculiar. It is not centered on the planet's geometric center and is tilted at 60 degrees to the axis of rotation. This results in a "wobbling" magnetic field that varies wildly in strength as the planet rotates. This oddity hints that the magnetic field is generated in a thin shell of electrically conducting liquid (likely a salty ocean) relatively close to the surface, rather than deep in the core.

Why is Uranus tilted? The leading theory is that it was struck by an Earth-sized object (or a series of smaller objects) early in its history, knocking it over.
